# Vietnam and Global High-Growth Business Opportunities for 2026 to 2030

## Executive synthesis

The medium-term opportunity set for 2026–2030 is unusually polarized. The strongest profit pools are forming where at least two structural forces overlap: AI adoption, aging populations, energy transition, regulatory complexity, or cross-border digital commerce. On that basis, the most attractive fields are not simply “big markets,” but markets where demand is rising faster than supply, compliance complexity creates defensibility, and software or service layers can sit on top of larger hardware or infrastructure capex. In practical terms, that puts agentic AI software, vertical SaaS, cybersecurity, digital payments, climate/adaptation software, digital health, energy storage, cross-border commerce infrastructure, and selected beauty/wellness niches at the top of the opportunity stack. citeturn24search12turn24search20turn23search3turn24search6turn34search5turn10search1turn26search17turn38search3

Vietnam stands out as one of the most attractive operating bases in Asia for 2026–2030 because it combines high macro growth with manufacturing integration and rapidly rising digital demand. The IMF now projects Vietnam’s GDP growth at **7.5% in 2026**, revised up on stronger technology exports and robust domestic demand, while the Google-Temasek-Bain *e-Conomy SEA 2025* report shows Vietnam’s digital economy reaching **$39 billion in 2025** and tracking toward **about $85 billion by 2030**; e-commerce alone is shown at **$25 billion in 2025** and around **$61 billion by 2030**. citeturn21view3turn21view1

Globally, the best sectors for new firms are no longer the same as the best sectors for large capital owners. Capital-heavy winners such as semiconductors, data centers, batteries, solar manufacturing, and EVs have enormous market size, but they are often poor fits for startups unless the entrant owns a specialized coatings, design software, testing, services, or component niche. By contrast, software-driven layers around AI, compliance, payments, healthcare workflows, and climate reporting can scale globally with much lower starting capital and better gross margins. Gartner estimates worldwide AI spending at **$2.52 trillion in 2026**, while worldwide IT spending is expected to reach **$6.15 trillion** and data-center-system spending **$653.4 billion** in 2026; those figures show where enterprise budgets are already moving. citeturn24search12turn24search1

## Macro backdrop and demand shifts

The macro backdrop is supportive for selective growth, but not for undifferentiated businesses. The IMF’s July 2026 update projects global growth at **3.0% in 2026** and **3.4% in 2027**, with world trade volume growth slowing to **3.5% in 2026** before recovering to **4.3% in 2027**. Advanced economies are expected to grow only **1.7% in 2026**, with the United States at **2.3%**, the euro area at **0.9%**, and Japan at **0.6%**. That means businesses selling measurable productivity, resilience, or compliance value should outperform businesses selling discretionary “nice to have” consumption without differentiation. citeturn21view3

The largest near-term business trend is the conversion of enterprise tech budgets toward AI infrastructure, AI models, and agentic workflow automation. Gartner estimates worldwide AI spending at **$2.52 trillion in 2026**, up **44% year over year**, and says the market for AI platforms and models alone will reach **$64 billion in 2026**, up **63.4%** from 2025. IDC’s spending guide still points to a more traditional AI market definition of **more than $631 billion by 2028**, which is lower because the scope differs; taken together, the data still points in the same direction: AI is moving from experimentation into operating spend. citeturn24search12turn24search20turn23search0

A second cross-market force is the re-regulation of digital business. Europe’s AI Act entered into force in 2024 and becomes broadly applicable from **2 August 2026**, with phased exceptions; Gartner also projects sovereign cloud IaaS spending at **$80 billion in 2026**, up **35.6%** from 2025. This combination supports high-growth demand for AI governance, model monitoring, privacy tooling, sector-specific compliance software, sovereign cloud integration, and cybersecurity. In other words, regulation is no longer only a cost center; it is becoming a revenue engine for vendors that can productize compliance. citeturn18search0turn24search6turn23search19

Consumer behavior is also changing in ways that favor specific categories. McKinsey’s 2026 consumer work says travel experiences grew **4.4% from 2023 to 2025**, while the 2026 Worldpay report shows digital wallets represented **56% of global e-commerce spend in 2025** and more than a third of in-person transaction value. McKinsey also continues to size the global wellness market at about **$2 trillion**, and its 2026 beauty research keeps the beauty market on track to reach **$590 billion by 2030**, growing around **5% annually**. The implication is straightforward: convenience, trust, health, and emotional reward are still strong spending themes even in a slower macro environment. citeturn13search12turn26search17turn38search1turn38search3

Demographics remain one of the most durable demand drivers. In the EU, **22.0%** of the population was already **65 or older** on 1 January 2025. In the United States, the population aged 65+ reached **61.2 million** in 2024. In China, UNFPA notes the elderly population could reach **358 million by 2030**; WHO China also highlights that people over 60 are projected to reach **28%** of the population by 2040. Aging is not just a healthcare story: it raises demand for home care, remote monitoring, supplement and wellness products, senior housing services, medical devices, robotics, and labor-saving automation. citeturn18search1turn17search7turn16search12turn16search1

The energy transition is creating both massive capex markets and large software-and-services spillovers. BloombergNEF says global energy-transition investment hit a record **$2.3 trillion in 2025**. The IEA says solar investment alone is expected to reach **$450 billion in 2025**, and its battery outlook says battery storage must rise roughly **14-fold to 1,200 GW by 2030** in the NZE pathway; BNEF expects **158 GW / 459 GWh** of storage deployments in **2026**, up about **41%** from 2025. For founders, the important point is that many attractive opportunities sit around the capex itself: design software, operations software, predictive maintenance, financing, recycling, siting, permitting, and supplier ecosystems. citeturn9search13turn10search4turn10search1turn9search9

Vietnam and Southeast Asia add another strong vector: digital catch-up plus supply-chain realignment. ASEAN’s digital economy is often cited at around **$300 billion** today and could reach **$1 trillion by 2030**, with a path to **$2 trillion** if the Digital Economy Framework Agreement is fully implemented. ASEAN also launched its **Digital Masterplan 2030** to guide regional cooperation from 2026 to 2030. Vietnam sits in the middle of that shift, combining electronics exports, platform consumption, and a still-underpenetrated B2B digitization base. citeturn14search6turn14search3turn21view2

### What the table implies

For founder-led ventures, the best balance of growth, margins, and entry cost is concentrated in **agentic AI**, **vertical SaaS**, **cybersecurity**, **regtech**, **payments infrastructure**, **digital health workflows**, **climate software**, and **commerce enablement**. For larger SMEs or family offices with more capex tolerance, **storage integration**, **retrofits**, **cold chain**, **water**, **beauty clinics/devices**, and **senior care services** become more attractive. ### Vietnam versus international markets

Compared with the U.S. and Europe, Vietnam offers lower starting costs and faster digital catch-up, but smaller enterprise deal sizes and thinner specialist talent pools. Compared with China, Vietnam offers friendlier white-space opportunities for SMEs and foreign partnerships, but a much smaller domestic market. Compared with Japan and Korea, Vietnam is earlier in healthcare digitization, cold chain, and B2B digitization, which means slower monetization in some cases but a better opportunity to shape the market rather than merely compete inside it. citeturn21view1turn29search2turn15search10turn19search3turn15search1

## Profit pools, niches, and trend status

### Where profit pools are deepest

The sectors with the highest **revenue potential** are AI, cloud/data centers, semiconductors, EVs, wellness, beauty, e-commerce, and the care economy. ### Evidence-based action recommendations

For a **startup**, the highest-probability path is to build a software or service layer attached to one of five structural wedges: AI workflow automation, compliance, aging care, digital commerce infrastructure, or clean-energy operations. For a **Vietnam-based SME**, the strongest strategy is usually to sell into export ecosystems—supplier software, trade documentation, cold chain, payments, traceability, care services, or wellness brands with regional reach. For a **larger capital holder**, the best opportunities shift to storage integration, retrofits, water, specialized manufacturing services, and platform-backed care services.
